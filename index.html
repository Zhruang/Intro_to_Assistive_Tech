week vi

Project Documentation W6

This week’s objective is basically to finalize the design in Fusion. One big part that was yet to be included in the design last week was the installation of electronics (micro-controller & sensor) into the pedal. Given the benefits of using a digital distance sensor, the electronic parts could really be positioned anywhere as long as the sensor is able to read the distance between the pedal and the base. The solution I came up is shown in the illustration: electronics (an Arduino Nano 33 and a Sharp Digital Sensor) are positioned near the left edge of the pedal base so that the usb-c port could be easily exposed on the left side; a recess is created to half-house the electronics (I attempted to design the part in which the electronics could be almost fully enclosed but turned out to be unnecessarily complex); two adhesive rubber bumpers are placed each on one side of the base to provide some cushioning by the end of the pedal travel and also to create some breathing space in-between the two parts so that the electronics won’t come into contact with the upper pedal.

At this point, my pedal switch design is basically settled. Given the limited resources around, I am not yet able to produce a fully-functional 3d-printed model but only to make a couple of 3d renders for reference. Most parts of the Fusion model are designed with practicality in mind so one potentially will be able to produce a functional physical model with the fusion file.

week v

Project Documentation W5

Over the course of this week, I tried to actually put my thoughts together and started to make sketches along with some Fusion modeling as well. One major update on the overall design of the pedal switch that happened this week is the idea of making my pedal switch adjustable. For I am aiming for a more universal design, being able to put the switch into use under various circumstances and for various people would be a reasonable next step. So, both the angle (between the pedal and the base) and the force (coming from the tension of the spring) are designed to be adjustable in order to cater to different needs. Using the pedal while standing might require a smaller angle than using it while sitting in front of the desk, for instance; People don’t feel comfortable putting too much force into engaging with the switch may choose to use one with a lighter force. Such a feature is enabled by making the key mechanical component of the pedal—the spring—interchangeable. Each end of the spring is attached to a small cylinder base which itself is embedded with magnets. Round recesses into which the interchangeable springs could perfectly fit are created both on the base and the pedal, between the space where the spring would be in, and following the axis that runs from the front to the end of the pedal. The user will have freedom putting the interchangeable spring into different positions depending on the needs. The closer the spring is to the hinge, the more the pedal is opened up, and vice versa. I would also like to offer the option of having a set of springs that come with different forces to better fit the needs of different groups of people. On top of that, switching to a lighter spring while placing it closer to the hinge would provide the larger opening angle while maintaining a similar force feedback from the pedal, which is a nice implementation of both two customizable aspects.

week iv

Project Documentation W4

With queries about potential physical forms of my pedal switch, I went through some materials floating around on the Internet, and from the websites that Tamara kindly offered to me. I found one particular design that meets some of the qualities of the switch that I envisioned and thus could be referenced as a good starting point. He cut off two pieces of acrylic sheets as the pedal and the pedal base. Hinges are used on one side to connect the two pieces, and a switch and a strong spring on the other side to complete the basic yet functioning form of his pedal switch. Yet I am going for that progressive, analog output from the switch rather than an on / off, one simple switch could not be enough for the purpose and thus need to be replaced by something that can afford more. Digital distance sensor / Proximity sensor came to mind for I realized that I could totally free the sensor from the constraint of the pedal travel and pressure mechanism. Instead of using a pressure sensor that would lead to an excessive complexity of its mechanism, using a distance sensor that simple reads the distance between the pedal and the pedal base seems to me as a better option for then there is no worrying about whether stepping on the pedal too heavily would break the sensor and etc.

The potential use cases for my pedal switch will definitely be a focus over the next a few weeks. I haven’t yet found a ton of scenarios where such a switch would offer functionality that other on / off switch could not. However, what I do believe is that there still are great values in creating such a switch for there are few pedal switches, and adaptive switches in general, that could offer a continuous, progressive output currently on the market, and such a feature will definitely find places where it will fit the best. And, after all my pedal switch could just be used as a normal on / off switch as a last resort. Enough with excuses, now are a couple of use cases that I can think of at the moment: it could be used as a low-cost racing simulator pedal, or video game controller in general; it could be used to fine tune the color hue and brightness of some of the smart light bulbs on the market; could it be used to control the bathroom water faucet?; it could even be used as a musical instrument once you map the outputs signals onto a gamut of musical notes.

Writing Response W4

For me, the experience of seeing footages of the life at Camp Jened was ... shocking. I did not get as much as shock from simultaneously seeing that many disabled people like one of the counselors at Camp Jened once felt as said in the film, than from seeing the overflowing happiness and passion coming from everyone at the Camp. As a person who has lived his entire life in the city and “on the grid”, I could get enough anxiety just thinking about camping at somewhere in the countryside and not having an Internet connection and not having 30-minute food deliveries. I have become too spoiled by the so-called “modern life” to picture myself finding pleasure in sleeping in sheds or tents, fear of crabs, or unexciting diet. This resistance coming from myself speaks about the difference in the times and confrontation between city and countryside. What has the technologically advanced, highly efficient and seemingly orderly modern society turned me into? A person who is unable to adapt, and enjoy life outside the comfort zone carefully tailored by a larger culture. What was flourishing at the Camp Jened was the emancipation of individuality and solidarity among a group of people with strong relationships that eventually stimulate a tough struggle against discrimination and inequality then spanned decades. The strong spirit coming from the Camp is incredible and worthy of massive respect.

week iii

Writing Response W3

Britney Wilson in her essay “On NYC’s Paratransit, Fighting for Safety, Respect, and Human Dignity” has carefully pictured to us a staggering amount of issues around the running of Access-A-Ride in New York City—inconsiderate services from Access-A-Ride drivers, inefficient communication between passengers and drivers, unreasonable policies, and bad routings around the city, and so on. If someone stands aside and examines the New York City public services and city life in general, one may easy get to the conclusion that: yes, Access-A-Ride service is bad; but it’s not much different from any other aspects of the city life that at any minute could lead people into frustrations—expensive taxis, endless traffic jams, never-on-time subways, enraged pedestrians. If you follow this direction and think, well, Access-A-Ride itself has already been a practical social progress and a helpful service to fill the needs of tens of thousands of people in the city.

The real problem, however, lies underneath. It is the society’s attitude towards people in need of this service, usually people that are disabled in one way or another. Then you see rude and offensive drivers complaining about the troubles disabled people have given to them, and the society, as depicted in Wilson’s story. As opposed to consider themselves as people who put real action into helping people in need (the idea itself could lead to problematics for many of the difficulties disabled people would bump into on a daily basis are fundamentally caused by the lack of consideration for and attention to disabled people), these drivers think their passengers as unashamed people who take advantage of and are wasting the public resources.

Wilson has made a rule for herself that in most cases she would only file Access-A-Ride complaints that attack on the larger system and policies “unless an individual driver’s behavior represents a larger systemic problem”. However many of the annoying and disturbing drivers she met over the years, Wilson is rational and sane enough to pull herself out and try to foster improvements on a systematic level. Yet, in the second half of her essay, she tells the story of a driver peeing into a cup in front of her on the bus, with only two of them. It is annoying and disturbing? 200% percent. Does this behavior represents a larger systemic problem in Access-A-Ride? Not accurately. This behavior represents an even larger problem that has to do with the society’s attitude towards minorities and this attitude is manifested by the conduct of any individual. The peeing driver is an “enforcer” of many of the problematics of the society—not seeing disabled people as equal; peeing in front of a lady on a bus as if it was totally normal suggests that he might not even see her as human but a neutralized and dehumanized existence, which could be said as a common attitude towards disabled people. While we realize the ill attitudes and preconceptions stem from a larger degree of social education and discourse, the fact that many of the direct difficulties and threats are put onto disabled people by one and another individual is often overlooked among debates around the more remote and institutional aspects of this issue.

week ii

Writing Response W2

One big takeaway from both the virtual field trip to ADA and the videos on Lynn Valley’s website is in fact surprisingly the promise of “Do-It-Yourself” which is coming back in fashion. As we feel feeble when looking at the jaw-dropping prices of adaptive devices commonly found on the market and facing designers’ monopolized taste-making, the concept of DIY can be a remedy to reclaim the control of personal life. Cardboard, the magic medium that ADA has long trusted and put into wide use, is without exaggeration the single best medium for DIY adaptive design. Hours before the second week’s class have I forgotten to prepare some cardboard for the class only to realize they are readily spotted lying at the corners of my home – packing boxes that come with deliveries on a daily basis. One may never be worried about running out of cardboard but to compliment on its rigidity and versatility.

As a person’s need can be so infinitely specifc and idiosyncratic, the person herself/himself or close family members can turn out to be the most appropriate designer candidate. With mass production, project launch and bureaucracy out of the way, and with any material at hand (cardboard obviously as a nice choice), one can make designs that adapt to personal needs just fine. In that, to me the biggest contribution of ADA and Valley’s website is their educational values. The videos of simple yet sufficient designs introduced by Lynn Valley, workshops held in ADA, and the vitual field trip to ADA as it is, are precious inspirations that advocate for mutual help and self-help within our respective communities.

The chapter “Feeling Meets Testing” in the book Design Meets Disability converses mainly around the concept of experience prototyping. A rather recently-recognized concept that centers around user experience in its development at the potential expense of other criteria within a larger project. Well responding to the aim of eliminating ambiguity in defining the term prototype and clarifying the roles of various types of prototypes in their respective fields, the essay suggests that in certain appropriate cases, the user experience and client’s direct (or almost direct) engagement in the ongoing project can be and should be foremost prioritized even if it requires faking certain mechanism or technology and making temporary compromises on other aspects of the design as long as the promise at the end could be realized.

The emphasis on experience prototyping in modern design, particularly in adaptive design lies in the fact that user experience has become indeed the dominant factor in determining the value of a piece of design and ties with the design’s fundamental purpose. Feedbacks retrieved from experience prototypes could potentially avoid waste of huge financial investment and numerous person-hours, and guide designers toward some more promising direction as early as possible.

Another important and compelling role of experience prototyping is to figure out “what is the question” aside from to figure out “what is the answer”. As illustrated in the second half of the chapter with a series of user cases, experience prototyping’s capability of stimulating introspections, or public discussions around social issues challenges against the idea of design being merely a tool for problem-solving and reveals its potential of pushing humanity forward.

The chapter “Identity Meets Ability” in the same book questions the existence of a clear distinction between “disabled” and “able-bodied”, adaptive design and mainstream design, and faults the binary philosophy underneath such definitions. “Every human being can experience some degree of disability.” This idea put forward by WHO may well be a guideline for designing for a wider audience, or to say, universal design. The chapter elaborates this idea even further as something that would sound like “every human being can experience some degree of disability depending on the context they are situated in.” It is the society and life situations that define one’s disability and ability. So commonly and easily have we connect the word “disabled” with the idea of physical impairment but in fact “activity limitation” and “participation restriction” as two manifestations of disability, which, yes, “impairment” may well lead to, in a broader view, can be seen as “a universal human experience”. On an imaginary spectrum of disability, almost everyone can find a position in any of its multiple dimensions. Take “visual impaired” as a example – people who are physically blind, people who are shortsighted, people who are shortsighted and accidentally forget to wear glasses, people whose eyes are occupied, people who don’t feel like having their eyes occupied, and many many other, can at some point be “visually impaired” and demand for adaptive designs that cater to this difficulty.

Disability may even extend beyond the realm of the human body. In this world that is so over-repeatedly referred to as “the digital age”, too many daily routines for someone in the city are taken well care of by personal digital devices and softwares. The street scenes in China, for instance, are absolutely bombarded with QR codes, which have become this omnipotent tunnel that links to virtually everything. From buying fruits in grocery stores to clarifying oneself as “Covid-free”, from ordering food in restaurants to reserving a museum ticket, QR code is the essence of modern life in China. This frightening craze for QR codes in China at any time will render anyone without a phone, or even without Wechat (the omnipresent social media / mobile payment / everything application created by the tech giant Tencent) as disabled, technically disabled. While many Chinese triumph over the convience and efficiency that the ubiquitous QR codes have enabled, the society’s heavy reliance on it is pushing people that have difficulty accessing mobile devices (the technically-illiterate and the financially-challenged in particular, but it could be anyone at some point really) into a extremely vulnerable and powerless position.

week i

Writing Response W1

Inspiration. Both Ben Shpigel’s essay and Stella Young’s TED talk put this word onto the central stage of debate. Inspiration, this seemingly uplifting word, has brought frustration to people who are considered as “disabled”. And, it’s never been the word’s fault, it sure is to be used to convey an encouraging and positive attitude. The problem surfaces however when the word inspiration is used when “normal” people try to fabricate a narrative around the “disabled” people to encourage another group of “normal” people. The majority in the society, the “normal”, is the sender and the recipient, the manufacturer and the consumer — the absolute center of this celebration carnival. Yet the “disabled” is put into the position of the consumable and the prey. This sickening narrative “[objectifies] one group of people for the benefit of another group of people”, said by Young. “Disabled” people have the chance to make their appearances in the public view nowadays largely because the dominant majority sees the potential in them as “objects of inspiration” that can in turn better serve the the majority of this society which actually disable the “disabled” and put them into difficult positions more than anything else. “Disabled” people are fundamentally othered according to an ever-present definition of normalcy and every bits of their virtues, achievements and humanity are thus evaluated differently which to a certain degree leads to the dehumanization of the “disabled” people.

So the next question is, who’s to define normalcy? I intentionally put quotation marks on the word “disabled” and the word “normal” every time they appear in the paragraph above for I believe that there is an urge to reflect on and re-evaluate our preconception of these two notions and to question the nature of normalcy. Asides from people who are more commonly classified as having disabilities, the elderly, shortsighted people, mid-aged employees who suffer from depression and anxiety, young kids, people who have lactose intolerance, just to name a few, may all run into all sort of difficult situations. And what’s most important, many many of the difficult situations and discriminations towards these people are fundamentally encouraged and bred by this society of low tolerance and ignorance.

In fact, most standards we tend to set as the normalcy across different fields are non-representative, to put it nicely, or faulty, to put it more accurately. A presumption that a person should have one head, two arms, two legs, should be able to do five push-ups, enjoy peanut butter, operate a smartphone and so on has made us narrow-minded. Apart from blunt discriminations, physical or verbal, towards people who fail to meet the society’s standard for normalcy, a broader and seemingly invisible discrimination emerges on a cultural level, and manifests itself most profoundly in field of industrial design, according to Sara Hendren. The modern industrial design, despite those “inspirational” promises that advanced technologies and design theories have given to us, has greatly failed in being aware of the different and personalized needs from different people in different physical and mental conditions and in turn accommodate for each. Most of the so-called “human factors design” data put into the ergonomic design are almost solely based on healthy, young, white males. This monopolized kind of human condition and aesthetics might even recall Walter Benjamin’s theory of “the aestheticization of politics” which in Benjamin’s words was the key ingredient to the fascist regimes. In order to combat this dangerous intolerance, the next step should be for us to advocate for an open mind that can accept different people as being different, and to be more democratic and inclusive in the designing and decision-making process.
